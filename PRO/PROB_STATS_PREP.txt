2) KEY DEFINITIONS (SHORT VIVA ANSWERS)
- Random variable: a variable whose values depend on outcomes of a random process.
- Population vs sample: population is the full set; sample is a subset used to estimate.
- Mean: average of sample values; estimator of population mean.
- Variance/standard deviation: spread of data around the mean.
- Conditional probability: probability of A given B, written P(A|B).
- Confidence interval: range of plausible values for a population parameter.
- Correlation: measures linear association; does not imply causation.

3) FORMULAS WE USED
- Sample mean: x_bar = (1/n) * sum(x_i)
- Sample standard deviation: s = sqrt( sum((x_i - x_bar)^2) / (n - 1) )
- 95% CI for mean (unknown sigma): x_bar +/- t_(alpha/2, n-1) * s / sqrt(n)
- Conditional probability: P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)
- Binomial PMF: P(X = k) = C(n,k) * p^k * (1-p)^(n-k)
- Normal tail: P(X > a) = 1 - Phi((a - mu)/sigma)

5) WHY WE USED T-DISTRIBUTION FOR CI
- Population standard deviation is unknown.
- Sample size is large but the correct formula for unknown sigma uses t.
- As n grows, t approaches the normal distribution.

6) HOW TO EXPLAIN THE PROBABILITY PARTS

- Binomial example: probability that exactly 7 out of 10 randomly selected patients have disease if p is the dataset target rate.
- Normal cholesterol model: assumes chol is approximately normal to estimate tail risk above 240.

7) LIKELY VIVA QUESTIONS + SHORT ANSWERS
Q: Why use confidence intervals?
A: To quantify uncertainty around the sample mean and show a plausible range for the population mean.

Q: Why use t-distribution instead of z?
A: Population standard deviation is unknown; t accounts for extra uncertainty.

Q: What does P(target=1 | male) mean?
A: The probability a patient has heart disease given they are male.

Q: What does P(chol > 240) represent?
A: The estimated probability of high cholesterol using a normal model.

Q: What does a correlation heatmap show?
A: The strength and direction of linear relationships between numeric variables.

Q: Why histograms and KDE?
A: To see the shape of distributions, skewness, and outliers.

Q: What is the binomial distribution used for here?
A: Modeling the probability of k successes (heart disease cases) in n independent trials.
